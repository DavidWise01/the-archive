The Injustice & the Legacy

He helped win a war and invent the computer — and his own country prosecuted him for being gay, took his clearance, and forced hormone treatment on him. He died at 41. This book is that injustice, the long reckoning that followed, and why his name now stands for both genius and a wrong made right too late.

Apology and pardon

In 2009 the Prime Minister apologised; in 2013 the Queen granted a posthumous pardon.

+1 his family argued a pardon for him alone wasn't enough — justice had to reach all who were convicted.

08

"Turing's Law" and the £50

A 2017 law pardoned thousands convicted under the old statutes; in 2021 his face went on the £50 note.

2017 Turing's Law · 2021 Bank of England note

so his name now anchors a broader justice and national honour.

+1 ~65,000 men were convicted under those laws; ~15,000 were still alive when the pardons came.

Why he still matters
  • He defined the computer in theory and helped win a war in practice — two achievements either of which would secure his place.
  • He framed the question of machine intelligence that still drives AI, and his name marks computing's highest award.
  • His persecution — convicted, chemically castrated, cast out — became a defining symbol of the injustice done to gay people under those laws.
  • The apology (2009), pardon (2013), Turing's Law (2017), and £50 note (2021) mark a reckoning that came decades too late.
  • He stands now as both the father of computer science and a reminder of what bigotry costs — the two are inseparable in his story.
How a legacy gets built — and the death debate
  • His conviction, punishment, and the official suicide ruling are documented fact. His genius and war role are beyond dispute.
  • But how he died is genuinely debated. Biographer Andrew Hodges reads it as suicide — a poisoned apple echoing his favourite fairy tale, Snow White. Scholar Jack Copeland argues it may have been accidental — cyanide fumes from a home experiment, and a poorly conducted inquest (the apple was never even tested for cyanide).
  • This series does not resolve it for you. Both readings come from serious people working from the same thin evidence — and the honest answer is that we don't fully know.
  • Beware accounts that state the cause flatly either way, or that treat the dramatized film as history — it invents characters and scenes.
  • The figures here — lives saved, men convicted, years shortened — are estimates and records, cited as such. Told straight, with the persecution named and the death left honestly open, his story needs no embellishment. It is devastating and luminous on its own.
