The Holevo Bound

the quantum frontier · pamphlet 3

A qubit feels like a vault — a whole sphere of possible states, a continuum of information. The Holevo bound is the lock on that vault: you can pack vast structure in, but you can pull at most one classical bit back out per qubit. This pamphlet is that hard, humbling limit.

One bit per qubit

The upshot: a single qubit yields at most one classical bit of recoverable information.

result the famous "one bit" ceiling

+1 this is the honest correction to every "a qubit holds infinite data" headline — it holds it, but won't hand it over.

The clever exception

With shared entanglement, "superdense coding" sends two bits in one qubit — bending, not breaking, the rule.

It guards secrets

Limiting what an eavesdropper can extract is exactly what makes quantum cryptography secure.

use bounding an adversary's accessible information

so the Holevo limit is a security feature, not just a constraint.

+1 "you can't read it all" is precisely the property you want when the reader is a spy.

The core of it
  • A qubit's state is a continuum, but storing information and retrieving it are different — measurement only ever returns one collapsed outcome.
  • Holevo's theorem (1973) proves a hard cap: the accessible classical information ≤ the von Neumann entropy (the Holevo χ quantity).
  • The upshot is the famous "one bit per qubit" ceiling — a qubit is not infinite classical storage.
  • It underpins quantum channel capacity and quantum cryptography (limiting an eavesdropper is a feature).
  • The clever exception, superdense coding, sends two bits in one qubit — but only by spending pre-shared entanglement, so the bound still holds.
Settled vs. frontier
  • Settled: Holevo's theorem and the one-bit ceiling are proven, standard results — among the most-cited in quantum information.
  • The "one bit per qubit" phrasing is exact for sending classical data without extra resources; superdense coding shows resources change the accounting — both facts are true, and not in conflict.
  • This is the key myth-corrector of the set: a qubit's richness is real, but its readable classical content is strictly limited. Hold both halves.
