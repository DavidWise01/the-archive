He did his great work inside the most creative lab of the century — and treated genius and juggling as the same impulse. This book is the world that made him: the golden age of Bell Labs, the war work, the wife who built machines with him, and a workshop full of beautiful, useless wonders.

Beating roulette

With Edward Thorp he built a tiny wearable computer to predict a roulette wheel.

+1 the same Thorp went on to use math to beat blackjack and then Wall Street — a lineage that starts here.

His place in it
  • His interest in switching circuits began at MIT, servicing the relays of Vannevar Bush's giant differential analyzer.
  • He did his landmark work at Bell Labs — the era's most fertile lab — where freedom to chase elegant problems produced the 1948 paper.
  • His wartime cryptography and fire-control work seeded his secrecy theory, and he met Alan Turing at Bell Labs in 1943.
  • His wife Betty, a numerical analyst, was a genuine collaborator on his home-built machines.
  • His workshop produced Theseus (a maze-learning mouse), Throbac, the "Ultimate Machine," juggling robots, and a wearable roulette computer — wonders built mostly for joy.
Solid vs. embellished
  • Solid: his Bell Labs and MIT careers, the inventions, the Turing meeting, and Betty's collaboration are all well-documented (notably in the biography A Mind at Play).
  • The eccentric stories are unusually reliable — corroborated by colleagues and family. Even so, retellings sometimes inflate the details, so treat specific embellishments (exact dates, who saw what) with mild care.
  • The "first wearable computer" label is reasonable but depends on definitions — others made claims too; it's a contender, not an undisputed crown.
  • His relays-to-theory path is real, but popular accounts can make it sound more linear than the messy reality of years of thinking.
  • Where this book reports an anecdote, it's well-attested — but the legend and the man are easy to blur, and the man is remarkable enough without polish.
