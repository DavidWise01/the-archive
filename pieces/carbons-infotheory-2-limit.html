Closing the gap
01

Turbo codes (1993)

Berrou and Glavieux stunned a conference with codes that nearly hit the Shannon limit.

who Claude Berrou & Alain Glavieux, France

so the 45-year wall fell — capacity was suddenly within reach.

+1 their claimed performance was so good that many experts first assumed it was an error.

02

The trick: iteration

Two simple decoders pass guesses back and forth, refining each other — "turbo" like an engine's feedback.

idea iterative, probabilistic decoding

so near-optimal decoding became practical, not just theoretical.

+1 turbo codes power 3G, 4G, and 5G — the breakthrough is in the phone in your pocket.

03

LDPC: ahead of its time

Robert Gallager invented low-density parity-check codes in his 1960 MIT PhD — then they were shelved.

why ignored 1960s hardware couldn't decode them

so a near-optimal idea sat unused for decades.

+1 Gallager lived to see them everywhere — winning prizes for work he'd done over 30 years earlier.

04

LDPC rediscovered (~1996)

Amid the turbo-code excitement, David MacKay and others realized Gallager's codes also nearly reach the limit.

bonus LDPC is unpatented — free to use

so it spread into Wi-Fi, DVB-S2, 5G, and Blu-ray.

+1 being patent-free is exactly why open standards favored LDPC over the patented turbo codes.

Spreading out
05

Algorithmic information

Kolmogorov (and others) asked: what's the shortest program that could generate this data?

idea complexity = length of the shortest description

so "information" gained a second, computational meaning.

+1 it links Shannon's entropy to the very notion of randomness — random means incompressible.

06

Relative entropy (KL divergence)

Kullback and Leibler measured how far one probability distribution sits from another.

tool KL divergence

so statistics gained an information-based notion of "difference."

+1 this single quantity becomes the bridge to modern machine learning in Book 3.

07

Mutual information

A measure of how much knowing one thing tells you about another.

idea shared information between variables

so fields could quantify dependence without assuming a formula.

+1 neuroscientists use it to ask how much a neuron's firing "tells" about a stimulus.

08

The Bandwagon warning

As information theory spread into psychology, biology, and economics, Shannon urged caution.

essay "The Bandwagon" (1956)

so he reminded everyone it wasn't a cure-all for every field.

+1 a founder reining in his own hype — the careful applications endured, the overreaches faded.

The story so far
  • Turbo codes (1993), by Berrou and Glavieux, were the first practical codes to closely approach the Shannon limit, using iterative decoding — and now run 3G/4G/5G.
  • LDPC codes, invented by Gallager in 1960 but ignored, were rediscovered around 1996; near-optimal and patent-free, they spread into Wi-Fi, 5G, and Blu-ray.
  • Modern codes now get within about 1 dB of the limit — so close that research has shifted to making decoding faster and more adaptive.
  • Meanwhile the idea spread beyond communication: algorithmic (Kolmogorov) complexity, KL divergence, and mutual information reached statistics, neuroscience, and economics.
  • Shannon's own "Bandwagon" (1956) warned against overreach — and the disciplined uses are what lasted.
How we know — and the honest caveats
  • The turbo/LDPC story, dates, and people are well-documented (MIT's own histories, the original 1993 ICC paper, Gallager's 1960 thesis).
  • "Within ~1 dB" and "closely approach" are real but channel-dependent — performance varies with code length, noise, and conditions; no practical code reaches the limit exactly.
  • Turbo vs. LDPC isn't a simple winner — they're competing families with different trade-offs; standards pick by latency, complexity, and licensing.
  • The "spread into other fields" is genuine but uneven — some applications are rigorous, others were the very overreach Shannon cautioned against. Treat sweeping "information theory explains X" claims with care.
  • Book 3 carries the idea to its current frontiers: quantum information and modern AI.
