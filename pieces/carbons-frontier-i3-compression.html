Prediction is Compression

the intelligence frontier · pamphlet 7

Here is the idea that ties the whole series into a knot: to predict the next symbol well is to compress the data well — they are, provably, the same skill. It runs from Shannon's bits to Book 2's Kolmogorov complexity, and it's why some argue that learning to compress just is learning. This pamphlet is that equivalence — and its honest limits.

The core of it
  • Predicting the next symbol well and compressing data well are the same skill — provably, via the bits-of-surprise accounting of pamphlet 6.
  • Arithmetic coding is the concrete bridge: feed it a predictor's probabilities and it produces near-optimal compression.
  • At its deepest, this is Kolmogorov complexity (Book 2) — compression as finding the shortest description — and the basis of Solomonoff prediction.
  • So a language model is a compressor; recent work shows large models can even out-compress dedicated tools.
  • The Hutter Prize turns this into an AI benchmark, and some argue "intelligence is compression" — a serious, suggestive hypothesis.
Settled vs. hypothesis
  • Settled: the prediction-compression equivalence is rigorous and standard — it's basic information theory, and the LLM-as-compressor result is demonstrated.
  • A genuine hypothesis, not a law: "intelligence is compression" is a real research idea that remains contested. It's suggestive and partly formalized (Solomonoff, Hutter), but not proven — treat it as a live claim, not established fact.
  • Compression captures pattern-finding and prediction, which are part of intelligence — but reasoning, goals, embodiment, and understanding may not reduce to it. Skeptics push exactly here.
  • "Lossless compression of text" is a precise technical claim; don't over-read it as the model "understanding" in a human sense (Book 0's caution).
