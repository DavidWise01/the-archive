Enheduanna

Before any name we know, hers. Around 2300 BCE, a high priestess in the city of Ur pressed her name into wet clay and claimed her own writing — becoming the earliest author in human history known by name. This book is who she was, and why that matters.

Who she was
01

A name that means her office

"Enheduanna" isn't a birth name — it's a Sumerian title she took on appointment.

means roughly "high priestess, ornament of heaven" (En · hedu · Ana)

so her very name marks the role she held.

+1 she was likely born with a Semitic Akkadian name; the Sumerian title came when she moved to Ur.

02

Daughter of the first empire

Her father Sargon united Sumer and Akkad into the earliest empire we know of.

reign Sargon, c. 2334–2279 BCE

so she was royalty at the hinge of a new political order.

+1 placing his daughter over Ur's chief temple was a political move as much as a sacred one.

03

High priestess of the moon

Sargon appointed her chief priestess of Nanna, the moon god, at Ur.

role entu — high priestess

so she held religious, political, and economic power at once.

+1 by several accounts she held the office around 40 years — a long, formidable tenure.

04

The first we can name

Older writing exists — but anonymous. Enheduanna is the first author whose name we know.

claim earliest named author in world history

so authorship itself, as a signed act, begins with her.

+1 she predates Homer by roughly 1,500 years — the usual "first poet" of the West.

Why she mattered
05

She signed her work

She didn't just write — she named herself as the maker, the radical part.

act "The compiler of these tablets is Enheduanna."

so she claimed responsibility and credit — the birth of authorship.

+1 to sign a work is to stand ready for credit or blame — a genuinely new idea in 2300 BCE.

06

A bridge between peoples

Her task was to weave Akkadian and Sumerian gods into one shared religion.

job blend two pantheons to bind an empire

so her writing was statecraft as much as devotion.

+1 shared religious culture was how a sprawling early empire held together — and she built it in verse.

07

The first inner voice

She wrote in the first person about her own fears, exile, and hopes.

first a recorded individual consciousness

so hers may be the earliest account of a person's inner life.

+1 "I, Enheduanna" — she put herself into the text, not just behind it.

08

Echoes for millennia

Her hymns were copied and studied for 500+ years after her death.

legacy models for later prayer and psalm

so faint echoes reach the Hebrew psalms and Homeric hymns.

+1 scribes kept recopying her work for centuries — ancient proof of how highly she was held.

The core of it
  • Around 2300 BCE, in the Sumerian city of Ur (now southern Iraq), a woman served as high priestess of the moon god Nanna.
  • She was a princess — daughter of Sargon of Akkad, founder of the world's first known empire — and her post was sacred and political at once.
  • She wrote hymns and poems, and she did the unprecedented thing: she put her name to them.
  • That makes her the earliest author known by name in human history — about 1,500 years before Homer.
  • She wrote in her own voice, about her own life — exile, restoration, devotion — which is why she still reaches us across more than four thousand years.
What we know — and how
  • Her existence is well-established: there are ancient records of her, two seals naming her servants, and a carved alabaster disk bearing her image and name, dug up at Ur in 1927 (now at the Penn Museum).
  • Her poems survive in copies — many of them — but the oldest surviving tablets date to around 1750 BCE, some 600 years after she lived.
  • Because we have copies, not originals, some scholars question how much she personally composed versus what later scribes shaped. This is a real, ongoing debate — and an honest part of her story, not a flaw in it.
  • What's not in dispute: she was a real historical person, of enormous standing, to whom the first signed authorship is attributed. (Books 1–3 take up her works, her world, and her legacy in turn.)
  • Spelling varies — Enheduanna, Enheduana, En-hedu-ana — all the same person; the cuneiform is 𒂗𒃶𒌌𒀭𒈾.
