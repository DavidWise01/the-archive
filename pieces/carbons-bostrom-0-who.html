The core of it
  • Nick Bostrom (b. 1973, Sweden) is a polymath philosopher — trained across physics, neuroscience, logic, AI, and philosophy, with a PhD from the LSE.
  • At Oxford he founded and led the Future of Humanity Institute (2005–2024), building an institution around humanity's long-term future.
  • He pioneered the concept of existential risk (2002) and wrote the bestseller Superintelligence (2014), helping launch the AI-safety field.
  • His most famous work is the simulation argument (2003) — note the title is a question, not a claim.
  • His gift throughout: turning vast, vague worries into sharp, analyzable structure.
How we know — and what's settled
  • Settled biography: his dates, training, FHI directorship, and major publications are well-documented (his own CV, Oxford/FHI pages, standard references).
  • Bostrom is a working, contemporary thinker, not a closed historical figure — his views evolve, and this series describes his ideas, which others vigorously contest.
  • He is a polarizing figure: hugely influential, also sharply criticized — both the simulation argument and his broader longtermism draw serious pushback (Book 3).
  • "Most-cited philosopher under 50" comes from his own materials; influence isn't endorsement — being widely read says nothing about being right.
  • This is the life; Book 1 is the argument itself, stated precisely and with its real limits.
