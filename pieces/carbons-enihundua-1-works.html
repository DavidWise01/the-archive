The Words She Left

She left hymns of real power — to a goddess, to the temples of an empire, and about her own exile and return. At least five poems carry her name. This book is what she actually wrote, and the line that changed everything.

The masterwork
01

The Exaltation of Inanna

A 153-line hymn — her best-known work — praising the goddess, then turning intensely personal.

name Nin-me-sara ("Lady of all the divine powers")

arc 65 lines of praise, then her own voice and plea.

+1 the poem had no ancient title — "Exaltation of Inanna" is a modern label scholars gave it.

02

Exile in her own words

She describes being driven from her office by a usurper, and her anguish.

villain a usurper named Lugalanne

so the hymn is also testimony — a first-person account of a coup.

+1 it's startlingly raw: she writes of disrespect, even what reads as a violation — not abstract piety.

03

The plea and the return

She calls on Inanna to restore her — and the poem ends in triumph as she reclaims her place.

turn appeal to the goddess → restoration

so devotion and survival are the same act in the text.

+1 she credits the goddess, not the king, with her restoration — a pointed choice of power.

04

"I gave birth to this song"

Near the end she reflects on composing the hymn itself — writing about her own writing.

move the poem turns to look at its own making

so she's conscious of authorship as she performs it.

+1 a poet writing about the act of composing — in 2300 BCE — is breathtakingly modern.

The wider body
05

The 42 Temple Hymns

A sweeping set of hymns, each to a temple in a different city across the realm.

scope temples of ~36 cities, Sumer and Akkad

so it's a spiritual inventory of a whole civilization.

+1 reconstructed from dozens of tablets — by binding every city's temple in one cycle, it bound the empire.

06

The Inanna hymns

Inninsagurra ("the great-hearted mistress") and Inninmehusa ("goddess of the fearsome powers").

subject Inanna in her fullness — love and war

so she built the earliest written portrait of a goddess.

+1 she venerated Inanna above all — unusual for a priestess officially serving the moon god Nanna.

07

The fragments

Two more poems attributed to her survive only in pieces.

state incomplete tablets

so her full output was likely larger than what reached us.

+1 what we have is the surviving fraction — absence of a tablet isn't absence of a poem.

08

Hymns as a new craft

She's credited with shaping the very forms of hymn, psalm, and prayer.

legacy paradigms later writers reused

so she didn't just write hymns — she helped invent the genre.

+1 her petitionary-prayer form echoes forward into biblical psalms and Homeric hymns.

The line that changed everything
"The compiler of these tablets is Enheduanna. My king, something has been created that no one has created before." — closing the Temple Hymns (modern translation; phrasing varies by translator)
Her body of work
  • Her masterwork is the Exaltation of Inanna (Nin-me-sara), 153 lines: praise of the goddess, then a first-person account of her exile and restoration.
  • Her widest work is the 42 Temple Hymns — one for the temple of city after city — a single cycle that gathered an empire's gods into shared verse.
  • She wrote further hymns to Inanna (Inninsagurra, Inninmehusa) and two surviving fragments.
  • She closed the Temple Hymns by naming herself the compiler and claiming the work was new — the first signed claim of authorship we possess.
  • She is credited with shaping the forms of hymn, prayer, and psalm that later traditions inherited.
Reading her today
  • We read her in translation from Sumerian, reconstructed from many clay tablets — so exact wording varies between translators. Any quote is a rendering, not her literal line.
  • Her complete poems were first published in one English translation in 2023 — astonishingly recent for the first author in history.
  • The surviving copies date to roughly 1750 BCE, ~600 years after she lived, which is why authorship is debated (Book 0) — but the works themselves are real and studied.
  • Much of her work is freely readable via the Electronic Text Corpus of Sumerian Literature, and in translations by scholars like Hallo & van Dijk, Meador, and Sophus Helle.
  • Book 2 sets these words back into the world that made them; Book 3 follows their long afterlife.
